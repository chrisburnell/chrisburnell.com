## February: Vaporwave (in review)

Unfortunately, February was a difficult month, as my family and I went through the loss of a family member. As a result, I wasn’t in a particularly adventurous mindset, and I fell back on familiar and comforting music rather than explore and learn more about music.

In a way, it’s probably a good thing that I picked *Vaporwave* for this month because I already listen to the genre regularly, so I don’t have an overwhelming sense of having <q>failed</q> this little challenge already at the second month of twelve.

I will probably end up taking a few hours during March to go back and find new stuff to listen to throughout the year and educate myself further on the (frankly) complex history of this scene and its lineage.

All that being said, [Bandcamp Friday](https://isitbandcampfriday.com/) took place on the 6<sup>th</sup> of this month, so I took advantage of the event to purchase a number of fun Vaporwave albums. I haven’t had the opportunity to properly go through them all (yet), but the album [ENCARDIA '99](https://slowerpace.bandcamp.com/album/encardia-99) by [slowerpace <span lang="ja">音楽</span>](https://slowerpace.bandcamp.com) was a stand-out for me not just because it so strongly epitomises the Vaporwave sound, but also because it sounds as if someone translated the [Encarta 99 Encyclopedia](https://archive.org/details/MSEncarta99) directly into Vaporwave music, with amazing track titles like *LEARNING TIME* and *FORCES OF NATURE*.

### Vaporwave, a short history

*… at least, as told by me. I might get some stuff wrong, but I have no interest in having debates about it—feel free to send me corrections, if you want. I might read 'em.*

As far as I understand, like most genres of music, the <q>origin</q> is both difficult and contentious to pinpoint. By and large, music changes through evolution, and to gaze at the timeframe of a given genre <q>birth</q> would show a blurry, watercolour gradient of hues and tones. Sometimes you can maybe discern a shape in the wash of colours (these are the artists/albums/tracks that *really* pushed the needle), but more often than not, there is no pinpoint that definitively marks the *start* of a new genre of music.

When it comes to Vaporwave, there is a **strong** case to be made that its origins are too murky to call a start time on, but there’s an **equally strong** case to be made for pinpointing <q>the start of Vaporwave as we know it</q>:

In the late 2000s, as the Internet matured (the Internet itself, not its denizens <span class="silly">lol</span>) towards a new decade, there were a few genres like chillwave and <abbr title="hypnogogic pop">h-pop</abbr> that were already using retro sounds, lo-fi aesthetics, and playing on nostalgia and a wistfulness for a half-remembered past.

It was out of this primordial soup that Vaporwave grew. One of the key moments in this early time was in October of 2011 when [James Ferraro](https://jamesferraroarchive.bandcamp.com) released his album, [Far Side Virtual](https://jamesferraroarchive.bandcamp.com/album/far-side-virtual). In it, James Ferraro masterfully blends ringtones, elevator music, and the <q>sound</q> of corporate technology and techno-optimism from the 80s, 90s, and early 2000s to evoke an uncanny dreamscape of consumerism and overwhelming nostalgia (as a kid of the 90s, at least).

Unbeknownst to the world, as the genre bubbled slowly to life in underground, indie circles, the artists that were pushing the envelope in this scene were creating a *supersaturated solution*. All the elements were there, in suspension, just waiting for the right conditions.

On the 9<sup>th</sup> of December, 2011, [Vektroid (as Macintosh Plus)](https://vektroid.bandcamp.com) released [Floral Shoppe](https://vektroid.bandcamp.com/album/floral-shoppe), crystallising *Vaporwave* in these underground circles, and, in a way, helped to define the <span lang="fr">je ne sais quoi</span> that makes up what Vaporwave *is*: 80s/90s Japanese city pop, corporate <abbr title="branded instrumental music, elevator music">muzak</abbr>, lo-fi and chopped and screwed samples, and subtly-critical or ironic <q>sounds</q> of late stage capitalism.

This is what gives Vaporwave its interesting but slightly-eerie quality: a world that was *sold* to us, but refracted through the uncynical, wide-eyed wonder of childhood, before we knew enough to be critical, when everything just seemed so shiny and glossy and exciting and *new*.

Of course, nothing happens in a vacuum, and the Internet at large quickly caught onto this new but nostalgic genre of music. Maybe the conditions were *just right*, because it exploded in popularity throughout 2012, and I think this probably has a lot to do with how <abbr title="how easily something can be made into a meme">memeable</abbr> the Vaporwave aesthetic was and that the nostalgia factor of Vaporwave is targeted at the demographic of people who are more likely to make memes about things in the first place.

From there, genres and sub-genres and sub-sub-genres (that some would argue are genres unto themselves, thank you very much) would grow and blossom through their own scenes and wider circles that were developing.

There are far too many genres that were born from this time and from very similar origins for me to even name, and it’s been interesting to see and hear how Vaporwave has evolved and splintered in the ~14 years since *Far Side Virtual* and *Floral Shoppe* came out.

## March: Baroque/Classical/Romantic

I spent a lot of my youth learning piano, mostly baroque and classical pieces, and have been trying to pick it back up as an adult. My parents are both big fans of these genres, and I’ve been very familiar throughout my life (as a result), so this month, again, will probably be more about the depth of my exploration rather than breadth.

I’d really love to get a stronger understanding of what defines these genres (usually lumped together under the umbrella of <q>classical music</q>), what kind of influence history had on this music, and deep dive on some of my favourite aritsts/composers of these periods.

*I’m definitely going to be listening to and playing a lot of Beethoven this month!*
